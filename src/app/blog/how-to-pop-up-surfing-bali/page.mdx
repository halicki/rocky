<h2 className="font-heading text-2xl font-bold text-white mt-10 mb-4">How to Pop Up on a Surfboard: The Movement, Step by Step</h2>

<p className="text-text-secondary leading-relaxed mb-4">
  The pop-up is one continuous motion that takes about a second, but it helps to see it as five moments. Learn them slowly, then run them together until the seams disappear.
</p>

<ul className="space-y-4 text-text-secondary mb-6">
  <li className="flex gap-2 items-start"><span className="text-accent-blue mt-1">1</span><span><strong className="text-white">Paddle and commit.</strong> Lie centred, chest slightly lifted, and paddle hard. Keep going two or three strokes past the point where you think you have the wave — you want to feel the board accelerate on its own before you move.</span></li>
  <li className="flex gap-2 items-start"><span className="text-accent-blue mt-1">2</span><span><strong className="text-white">Hands flat under your chest.</strong> Plant both palms on the deck beside your ribs, roughly where a push-up would start. Never grip the rails — hands on the rails is the single fastest way to tip yourself sideways.</span></li>
  <li className="flex gap-2 items-start"><span className="text-accent-blue mt-1">3</span><span><strong className="text-white">Press up, eyes forward.</strong> Straighten your arms and lift your chest, looking at the beach and not at the board. Where your eyes go, your weight follows; look down and you go down.</span></li>
  <li className="flex gap-2 items-start"><span className="text-accent-blue mt-1">4</span><span><strong className="text-white">Swing the back foot through.</strong> With your weight in your hands, sweep your back foot through the gap under your hips and plant it across the stringer over the tail. The front foot follows underneath you, landing between your hands.</span></li>
  <li className="flex gap-2 items-start"><span className="text-accent-blue mt-1">5</span><span><strong className="text-white">Release and sink low.</strong> Let go of the board, bend your knees deeply, keep your chest up and your arms out for balance. Low and stable beats tall and stiff every single time.</span></li>
</ul>

<p className="text-text-secondary leading-relaxed mb-4">
  The critical detail is that steps two to five happen as one movement, not five. Beginners who learn the pop-up as separate stages — push, pause, knee, pause, stand — end up with a movement that takes two or three seconds, and no wave waits that long. Speed here comes from the sweep of the back leg, not from arm strength.
</p>

<h2 className="font-heading text-2xl font-bold text-white mt-10 mb-4">Where Your Feet Actually Land</h2>

<p className="text-text-secondary leading-relaxed mb-4">
  Foot placement decides whether you ride or fall, and it is where most self-taught surfers quietly go wrong. Your back foot wants to land across the board over the tail, roughly above the fins, with the heel near one rail and the toes near the other. Your front foot lands about shoulder-width ahead of it, near the middle of the board, angled slightly forward. Both feet sit across the stringer — the line running down the centre of the deck — not along it. Feet pointing at the nose is a skateboard stance, and on a wave it locks your hips and kills your balance instantly.
</p>

<p className="text-text-secondary leading-relaxed mb-4">
  Which foot goes in front is simply your natural stance. Left foot forward makes you regular, right foot forward makes you goofy, and neither is better than the other. If you have never skated or snowboarded, have someone push you gently from behind while you stand relaxed: the foot you step out with to catch yourself is almost always your front foot. A coach will spot it within a couple of waves anyway. What matters is picking one and repeating it, because alternating stances in your first weeks stops the movement from ever becoming automatic. In a first lesson, this is one of the first things I check — the full walkthrough of how that session runs is in our guide to <Link href="/blog/what-to-expect-first-surf-lesson-bali" className="text-accent-blue hover:underline">what to expect from your first surf lesson in Bali</Link>.
</p>

<h2 className="font-heading text-2xl font-bold text-white mt-10 mb-4">The Five Pop-Up Mistakes I Fix Every Week in Canggu</h2>

<p className="text-text-secondary leading-relaxed mb-4">
  Across thousands of lessons the same short list comes up again and again. If your pop-up is not working, the cause is almost certainly on it.
</p>

<ul className="space-y-4 text-text-secondary mb-6">
  <li className="flex gap-2 items-start"><span className="text-accent-coral mt-1">🦵</span><span><strong className="text-white">Going through the knee.</strong> The big one. Pushing up onto one or both knees and then standing turns a one-second move into a three-second move. Keep your knees off the deck and sweep the back foot through instead.</span></li>
  <li className="flex gap-2 items-start"><span className="text-accent-coral mt-1">👀</span><span><strong className="text-white">Looking down at the board.</strong> Everyone does it, because that is where the scary part is. Your weight follows your eyes, so looking down drives the nose under. Pick a point on the beach and stare at it.</span></li>
  <li className="flex gap-2 items-start"><span className="text-accent-coral mt-1">✋</span><span><strong className="text-white">Grabbing the rails.</strong> Hands on the edges instead of flat on the deck tips the board on its side before you are even upright. Palms under the ribs, every time.</span></li>
  <li className="flex gap-2 items-start"><span className="text-accent-coral mt-1">⏱️</span><span><strong className="text-white">Popping up too early.</strong> Standing before the wave has genuinely taken the board leaves you stalling on the back of it. Feel the acceleration first, then go.</span></li>
  <li className="flex gap-2 items-start"><span className="text-accent-coral mt-1">🧍</span><span><strong className="text-white">Standing up straight.</strong> Legs locked, body tall, arms by your sides — and the first bump throws you off. Land in a crouch with bent knees and a low centre of gravity.</span></li>
</ul>

<p className="text-text-secondary leading-relaxed mb-4">
  What makes these stubborn is that none of them feel like the problem in the moment. From the inside it just feels like the wave was too fast or the board too wobbly. That gap between what you feel and what you actually did is why footage works so well: seeing your own knee touch the deck, or your eyes drop, ends the argument in a single frame. <h2 className="font-heading text-2xl font-bold text-white mt-10 mb-4">Practising the Pop-Up on the Sand</h2>

<p className="text-text-secondary leading-relaxed mb-4">
  There is a reason every lesson starts on the beach rather than in the water. On land there is no wave, no wobble and no clock, so you can build the correct shape at half speed and then gradually add pace. It is the cheapest improvement available to a new surfer and it costs nothing but ten minutes.
</p>

<p className="text-text-secondary leading-relaxed mb-4">
  Draw a board outline in the sand or lay a towel down, lie in the middle of it, and run ten repetitions. Watch three things only: hands flat beside your ribs, knees never touching, and both feet landing across the centre line rather than along it. Do that daily in the week before a surf trip and the movement will already be halfway automatic before you ever touch salt water. One honest caveat: solid ground is far more stable than a board floating on moving water, so expect your first attempts in the ocean to feel considerably harder than your best ones on the beach. That is normal, and it is not a sign the land practice was wasted.
</p>

<h2 className="font-heading text-2xl font-bold text-white mt-10 mb-4">From Whitewater Pop-Ups to Real Waves</h2>

<p className="text-text-secondary leading-relaxed mb-4">
  Most people stand up somewhere between wave four and wave ten of their first lesson, and after three to five sessions the pop-up stops being something you think about. That is when the goal quietly changes. On whitewater the board is already moving and the timing window is wide; on an unbroken green wave the drop is steeper, the face is moving underneath you, and the same pop-up now has to happen a beat earlier and considerably faster. Surfers are often surprised that a movement they had mastered suddenly falls apart the first time they paddle for a real wave.

<p className="text-text-secondary leading-relaxed mb-4">
