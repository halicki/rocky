<h2 className="font-heading text-2xl font-bold text-white mt-10 mb-4">Is Canggu Surfing Safe for Kids? The Direct Answer</h2>

<p className="text-text-secondary leading-relaxed mb-4">
  Yes. Canggu's main beginner break, <strong className="text-white">Batu Bolong</strong>, is a sand-and-reef bottom wave that rolls in slowly across a long, gradual seabed. The wave you teach kids on is the <em>whitewash</em> — already-broken foam moving in waist-deep water — not the green face further out. Whitewash is friendly: a kid can stand up in it, reset their feet, and try again without ever being more than a few metres from a coach.
</p>

<p className="text-text-secondary leading-relaxed mb-4">

Padang Padang and Bingin are the same. Even Kuta, which is also beginner-friendly, has more shore-break punch than Canggu's mellow rollers. For kids specifically, Canggu's combination of soft bottom, slow wave, and shallow practice zone is hard to beat.

<h2 className="font-heading text-2xl font-bold text-white mt-10 mb-4">What's the Right Age to Start?</h2>

<p className="text-text-secondary leading-relaxed mb-4">
  The honest minimum is around <strong className="text-white">age 6</strong>, and even then it depends on the kid. Three things matter more than the number on the birthday card:
</p>

<ol className="space-y-3 text-text-secondary mb-6 list-decimal list-inside">
  <li><strong className="text-white">Water confidence.</strong> Can the child put their face in seawater without freaking out? Can they swim 10 metres unaided? If yes, they're ready. If no, do a few pool sessions or beach play days first.</li>
  <li><strong className="text-white">Arm strength.</strong> Paddling a 7-foot foam board through whitewash takes more effort than it looks. A small 5-year-old often can't generate enough push to catch the wave themselves — the coach ends up doing all the work, and the child gets frustrated.</li>
  <li><strong className="text-white">Attention span.</strong> A surf lesson involves listening to instructions, waiting between waves, and trying again after wipeouts. Kids who can sit through a 30-minute story are fine. Kids who can't sit through 10 minutes will burn out by wave three.</li>
</ol>

<p className="text-text-secondary leading-relaxed mb-4">

From age 10 most kids can do the same 2-hour <Link href="/beginner-surf-lessons-bali" className="text-accent-blue hover:underline">beginner surf lesson</Link> as adults at the same level — they often catch on faster than their parents.

<p className="text-text-secondary leading-relaxed mb-4">

<ul className="space-y-3 text-text-secondary mb-6">
  <li className="flex gap-2 items-start"><span className="text-accent-blue mt-1">→</span><span><strong className="text-white">Surfboard impact.</strong> A loose foam board in whitewash can clock a kid in the head. Mitigation: leashes on every board, instructor positioned between the board and the child during paddle-out, and we keep beginner kids in their own zone away from intermediate surfers.</span></li>
  <li className="flex gap-2 items-start"><span className="text-accent-blue mt-1">→</span><span><strong className="text-white">Sun exposure.</strong> Two hours in equatorial sun melts pale skin. Long-sleeve UV rash guards are mandatory in my kid sessions, plus reef-safe SPF 50 reapplied at the halfway point. We also schedule kid lessons for early morning (7:30–9:00) when sun is gentler and the wind is offshore.</span></li>
  <li className="flex gap-2 items-start"><span className="text-accent-blue mt-1">→</span><span><strong className="text-white">Mild reef rash.</strong> Possible on the outside reef sections at very low tide, not on the sandy whitewash zone.   <li className="flex gap-2 items-start"><span className="text-accent-blue mt-1">→</span><span><strong className="text-white">Sea jellies.</strong> Wet-season (Dec–Feb) sometimes brings small jellyfish swarms.   <li className="flex gap-2 items-start"><span className="text-accent-blue mt-1">→</span><span><strong className="text-white">Fatigue and panic.</strong> The most common real issue. <h2 className="font-heading text-2xl font-bold text-white mt-10 mb-4">What Parents Actually Need to Do</h2>

<p className="text-text-secondary leading-relaxed mb-4">

<ol className="space-y-3 text-text-secondary mb-6 list-decimal list-inside">
  <li><strong className="text-white">Eat breakfast 90 minutes before</strong> — not 15 minutes before. Surfing on a stomach full of nasi goreng is a recipe for vomit. Toast and fruit at 6:30 for an 8:00 lesson works well.</li>
  <li><strong className="text-white">Apply reef-safe sunscreen 20 minutes before</strong> water entry, not at the beach (it washes off immediately). Reapply at the halfway break.</li>
  <li><strong className="text-white">Long-sleeve UV rash guard</strong> — non-negotiable for kids. Schools usually have loaners; if not, any beachside shop in Canggu sells them for 150–250k IDR.</li>
  <li><strong className="text-white">For under-10s, one parent in waist-deep water</strong> for the duration. You don't need to surf — just be there. Kids relax knowing you're 5 metres away.</li>
  <li><strong className="text-white">For 10+ kids, watch from a beanbag warung</strong> directly behind the lineup. Order a coconut. You'll see every wave they catch.</li>
  <li><strong className="text-white">Bring water and a snack for the post-surf hour.</strong> Kids crash hard after the adrenaline wears off. A coconut and a banana on the beach prevents the post-lesson tantrum.</li>
  <li><strong className="text-white">Don't compare them to siblings or you.</strong> Some kids stand up on wave one; others take three sessions. Both are normal.</li>
</ol>

<h2 className="font-heading text-2xl font-bold text-white mt-10 mb-4">Frequently Asked Questions</h2>

<h3 className="font-heading text-xl font-semibold text-white mt-8 mb-3">My child can't swim very well — should I still book a surf lesson?</h3>
<p className="text-text-secondary leading-relaxed mb-4">
  If they can put their face in water and float for 10 seconds, the answer is "probably yes, with a private coach in waist-deep water only." If they can't, do 2–3 pool sessions first to build basic water comfort. Surfing the whitewash doesn't require strong swimming, but panic in waist-deep water is a real thing for kids who haven't been in the ocean before.
</p>

<h3 className="font-heading text-xl font-semibold text-white mt-8 mb-3">What's the best month to bring kids to Canggu for surf lessons?</h3>
<p className="text-text-secondary leading-relaxed mb-4">
  May, June, September, and October are the sweet spot — clean dry-season conditions, smaller swells than peak July–August, and the water is warm year-round. December–February is wet season with bigger windswell and the occasional jellyfish swarm; doable for kids on small days but more weather-dependent. April and November are good shoulder months too.
</p>

<h3 className="font-heading text-xl font-semibold text-white mt-8 mb-3">Can I take my kid surfing without a coach if I surf myself?</h3>
<p className="text-text-secondary leading-relaxed mb-4">
  Honest answer: only if you've taught beginners before. Teaching is a different skill from surfing — knowing when to push them onto a wave, how to angle the board, where to position relative to other surfers. Most surfing parents find one coached lesson teaches the kid more than a week of dad-led whitewash. After that, supervised free-surf sessions with you in the water work fine.
</p>

<h3 className="font-heading text-xl font-semibold text-white mt-8 mb-3">Are there surf camps for kids in Canggu?</h3>
<p className="text-text-secondary leading-relaxed mb-4">
  A few, mostly running 5-day or week-long programs in school holidays. Quality varies wildly. If you're considering one, ask: what's the coach-to-kid ratio (should be 1:3 max), which beach, what time of day, and what's the back-up plan for big-swell days. A daily booked-coach approach often gives more flexibility for less money than a packaged camp.
</p>

<h3 className="font-heading text-xl font-semibold text-white mt-8 mb-3">My kid did one lesson and refused to go back. Normal?</h3>
<p className="text-text-secondary leading-relaxed mb-4">
  Completely normal. Some kids fall in love with surfing on day one, others need 2–3 lessons before it clicks, and a small handful decide it's not their thing. Don't force it. A single positive ocean experience is worth more than a forced second lesson. Try again in a year — kids' fear of waves often disappears in 12 months.
</p>

<h3 className="font-heading text-xl font-semibold text-white mt-8 mb-3">Do you offer family surf lessons where parent and kid surf together?</h3>
<p className="text-text-secondary leading-relaxed mb-4">
  Yes — my most popular kid format is actually a parent-child private. One coach, two boards, you and your child sharing the same lineup at Batu Bolong. Beginner parents often progress faster because they're showing off for the kid; the kid feels safer because mum or dad is right there. Two hours, both included, message me to set it up.
</p>
